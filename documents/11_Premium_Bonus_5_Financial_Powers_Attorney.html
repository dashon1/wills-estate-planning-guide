What Is a Power of Attorney?

Basic Definition

A Power of Attorney (POA) is a legal document that authorizes one person (the agent or attorney-in-fact) to act on behalf of another person (the principal) in financial or legal matters. The scope can be broad or very specific.

Key POA Terminology

  • Principal: The person granting the authority (you)
  • Agent (Attorney-in-Fact): The person receiving authority to act
  • Durable: Continues even if principal becomes incapacitated
  • Springing: Takes effect only upon incapacity
  • General: Broad authority over all financial matters
  • Limited (Special): Authority for specific transactions only
Why a Durable POA Matters

A regular (non-durable) power of attorney becomes invalid when the principal becomes incapacitated. A Durable Power of Attorney remains in effect, allowing your agent to manage finances during a period when you cannot. Without this document, your family may need to go to court to obtain a conservatorship, which is expensive, time-consuming, and public.

When POA Becomes Necessary

A Power of Attorney is essential when:

  • You become incapacitated due to illness or accident
  • You need someone to manage finances while traveling abroad
  • You want to plan ahead for potential incapacity
  • You have real estate or business interests requiring management
  • You need help with complex financial transactions

Which Type to Choose

Most estate planning experts recommend a General Durable POA that takes effect immediately. Springing POAs can cause delays during medical emergencies when family must first prove incapacity. The delay can cause serious financial harm during critical periods.

General vs. Limited Authority

General Durable POA grants authority to:

  • Access and manage bank accounts and investments
  • Buy and sell real estate
  • Pay bills, taxes, and manage debt
  • Handle insurance claims and benefits
  • Manage business interests
  • Make gifts and charitable donations
  • Handle legal matters

Limited POA Examples

  • Selling a specific piece of property
  • Managing a particular investment account
  • Handling real estate transaction while traveling
  • Managing business affairs during temporary absence
  • Signing documents at a real estate closing

Selecting Your Agent

Qualities to Look For

The person you choose as your financial agent should:

  • Be trustworthy and have your best interests at heart
  • Be organized and detail-oriented
  • Understand financial matters and be comfortable with money management
  • Be available and responsive
  • Live nearby or be easily reachable
  • Be able to handle stressful situations
  • Understand your values and priorities
  • Be willing to serve in this demanding role

Good Agent Candidates

  • Spouse or partner
  • Adult children
  • Siblings
  • Close friends of long standing
  • Professional trustee services

Consider Carefully

  • Anyone with financial disputes with you
  • Those who live far away
  • Those who might have conflicts of interest
  • Those uncomfortable with responsibility
Consider a Professional Agent

For complex estates, business owners, or when family dynamics make personal agents problematic, consider hiring a professional (bank trust department, trust company, or attorney) as your agent or co-agent. Professional agents charge fees but provide expertise and neutrality.

Designating Alternate Agents

Always name at least one alternate agent. If your first choice is unable or unwilling to serve, the alternate steps in. Consider naming two alternates in case the first alternate also becomes unavailable.

Agent Responsibilities and Duties

Fiduciary Duties

As your agent, the person has fiduciary duties, meaning they must:

  • Act in your best interest: Always prioritize your needs over their own
  • Follow your wishes: Make decisions consistent with your known preferences
  • Keep accurate records: Document all transactions and decisions
  • Not mix assets: Keep your property separate from their own
  • Disclose conflicts: Reveal any potential conflicts of interest
  • Provide accounting: Report to beneficiaries if required

Typical Financial Agent Tasks

  • Pay your bills and manage your checking account
  • Manage your investments and retirement accounts
  • File your tax returns
  • Collect your Social Security or pension benefits
  • Buy and sell securities
  • Manage real estate rentals
  • Maintain insurance policies
  • Handle government benefits applications
  • Make gifts to family or charities if authorized
Agent Limitations

Agents generally cannot:

  • Change your will or beneficiary designations
  • Make gifts to themselves (unless specifically authorized)
  • Perform illegal or improper transactions
  • Delegate authority without permission (in most states)
  • Create or modify trusts for you (in most states)

Implementation and Revocation

After Creating Your POA

  1. Store securely: Keep the original in a secure but accessible location
  2. Give copies: Provide your agent with a certified copy
  3. Notify key institutions: Give copies to your bank, investment firms, and financial advisors
  4. Update periodically: Review every 3-5 years or after major life changes
  5. Discuss with family: Make sure family members know you have a POA and where it is located

How to Revoke a Power of Attorney

You may revoke your POA at any time while you are competent:

  • Execute a written revocation document
  • Sign and date the revocation
  • Notify your agent in writing
  • Notify all institutions where the POA has been used
  • Retrieve any copies given to your agent
  • Create new POA if desired
Important Warning

If you become incapacitated without a durable POA in place, your family may need to petition a court for a conservatorship or guardianship. This process is expensive, can take months, requires ongoing court supervision, and creates a public record. It is far better to create a durable POA while you are healthy and competent.

When POA Ends

  • When you revoke it in writing
  • When you die
  • When the agent resigns or is unable to serve (unless alternate named)
  • When a specific end date is reached (for limited POAs)
  • When a court invalidates it
