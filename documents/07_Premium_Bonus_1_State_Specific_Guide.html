Key State Variations

California Requirements

Witnesses:2 competent witnesses
Self-Proving:Yes - attach notarized affidavit
Holographic:Yes, entirely handwritten, signed by testator
Community Property:Yes - spousal consent may be required
State Estate Tax:Exemption ~$12M, rates 0.8%-16%
Simplified Probate:Small estate affidavit for estates <$184,500

Texas Requirements

Witnesses:2 credible witnesses, 14+ years old
Self-Proving:Yes - notary recommended
Holographic:Yes, valid but witnesses recommended
Community Property:Yes - community property state
Independent Administration:Yes - court involvement minimized if selected
Statutory Wills:Texas recognizes form wills (dependent relative revocation)

New York Requirements

Witnesses:2 witnesses, 18+ years old
Self-Proving:Yes - recommended for probate ease
Holographic:Yes, subject to stricter interpretation
State Estate Tax:Exemption ~$6.9M, rates up to 16%
Surrogate's Court:Probate handled by Surrogate's Court in each county
Beneficiary Restrictions:Witness-beneficiaries may forfeit gift

Florida Requirements

Witnesses:2 witnesses, must sign in presence of testator
Self-Proving:Yes - attach notarized affidavit
Holographic:No - must be typed and witnessed
Homestead:Strong protections for surviving spouse
Simplified Probate:Disposition without administration for small estates
No Contest:Florida enforces no-contest clauses

Illinois Requirements

Witnesses:2 witnesses, 18+ years old
Self-Proving:Yes - attach notarized affidavit
Holographic:Yes, entirely handwritten and signed
State Estate Tax:Exemption ~$4M, rates up to 16%
Statutory Will:Illinois provides fill-in-the-blank will form

Important Note: This guide provides general information for each state. Requirements can change, and individual circumstances may affect which rules apply. For property in multiple states, wills in multiple jurisdictions, or complex family situations, consult with a licensed attorney in each relevant state.

Annual Verification: Estate planning laws change frequently. Verify current requirements for your state before executing any will. Consider subscribing to updates or consulting an attorney periodically.
