What Is a Trust?

Basic Definition

A trust is a legal arrangement where one person (the grantor) transfers property to another person (the trustee) to hold for the benefit of third parties (beneficiaries). Think of it as a container that holds assets with specific rules about who can access them and when.

Key Trust Components

  • Grantor: The person who creates and transfers assets to the trust
  • Trustee: The person or entity managing the trust assets
  • Beneficiaries: The people who receive benefits from the trust
  • Trust Document: The legal instrument creating the trust and its rules
  • Trust Assets: Property transferred into the trust
Why Trusts Matter

Unlike a will, which only takes effect at death and must go through probate, a trust can manage assets during your lifetime and after, potentially avoiding probate entirely, providing creditor protection, and ensuring proper distribution.

Common Types of Trusts

Living Trusts (Inter Vivos)

Created during the grantor's lifetime. Can be revocable or irrevocable. Primary benefit is avoiding probate by transferring assets to the trust.

Testamentary Trusts

Created through a will, takes effect at death. Used for managing assets for minors or ensuring continuing support for beneficiaries.

Special Purpose Trusts

  • Special Needs Trust: For disabled beneficiaries without affecting government benefits
  • Charitable Trust: For charitable giving with tax benefits
  • Generation-Skipping Trust: For passing wealth to grandchildren

Asset Protection Trusts

  • Domestic Asset Protection: Protect assets from creditors
  • Medicaid Trust: Protect assets for long-term care eligibility
  • Qualified Personal Residence: Protect primary residence

Common Scenario: A married couple creates a revocable living trust, funding it with their home, investments, and bank accounts. Upon death of the first spouse, the survivor becomes trustee and manages assets. After both die, the trust distributes to children according to the trust terms, avoiding probate.

When Trusts Are Appropriate

  • Own property in multiple states (avoids multiple probates)
  • Have minor children (ensures proper management)
  • Want to avoid probate (faster, more private)
  • Have significant assets (tax planning opportunities)
  • Special needs family member (government benefit preservation)
  • Blended families (protects both spouse and children)
  • Concerns about beneficiary mismanagement of inheritance

Key Consideration

A revocable living trust does not provide significant tax benefits or creditor protection during the grantor's lifetime. Its primary value is probate avoidance and privacy. For these benefits, an irrevocable trust or careful will planning may be more appropriate.

Trust Implementation Steps

Step 1: Decide Trust Type

Work with an estate planning attorney to determine which type of trust best fits your situation and goals.

Step 2: Create Trust Document

Work with an attorney to draft the trust instrument, defining terms, beneficiaries, trustee powers, and distribution rules.

Step 3: Fund the Trust

Transfer assets to the trust by changing ownership (e.g., from "John Smith" to "John Smith, Trustee of the Smith Family Trust"). This step is critical and often overlooked.

Step 4: Designate Trustees

Name primary and successor trustees. Consider professional trustees for complex situations.

Trustee Responsibilities

Duties of a Trustee

  • Fiduciary Duty: Act in the best interest of beneficiaries
  • Prudent Investor: Manage trust assets wisely
  • Record Keeping: Maintain detailed accounts of all transactions
  • Distributions: Make distributions according to trust terms
  • Communication: Keep beneficiaries informed as required
  • Tax Compliance: File trust tax returns and pay any taxes due

Professional vs. Individual Trustees: Banks, trust companies, and attorneys offer professional trustee services for a fee (typically 0.5-1.5% of trust assets annually). Consider this for complex estates, family conflicts, or if no suitable family member is available.

Costs and Considerations

Trust Creation Costs

  • Simple revocable trust: $1,500-$3,000
  • Complex irrevocable trust: $3,000-$10,000+
  • Attorney fees vary by complexity

Ongoing Costs

  • Annual trust administration: $500-$2,000
  • Tax preparation: $300-$1,500
  • Asset management fees (if applicable)
Important Note

Trusts are powerful but complex legal instruments. Professional guidance is strongly recommended. This guide provides education only - consult with an estate planning attorney to determine if a trust is appropriate for your situation and to properly draft and fund any trust.
