Understanding Healthcare Directives

What Are Healthcare Directives?

Healthcare directives are legal documents that specify your wishes regarding medical treatment if you become unable to make decisions for yourself. They ensure your voice is heard even when you cannot communicate.

Healthcare Power of Attorney

  • Names someone to make medical decisions on your behalf
  • Takes effect when you cannot decide for yourself
  • Can be broad or limited in scope
  • Can be revoked if you regain capacity
  • Applies only to healthcare decisions

Living Will (Advance Directive)

  • Documents your specific medical treatment wishes
  • Does not name a decision-maker
  • Focuses on end-of-life care preferences
  • Guides family and doctors in your absence
  • Cannot be changed once incapacitated
Why Both Are Recommended

Using both documents together provides comprehensive coverage. The Healthcare POA designates someone to make decisions, while the Living Will specifies what decisions should be made. Together, they ensure both who decides and what to decide is documented.

When Healthcare Directives Take Effect

Healthcare directives generally become active when:

  • A physician certifies that you are unable to make medical decisions
  • A mental health professional confirms incapacity (for psychiatric decisions)
  • You have been evaluated and found unable to understand your condition or treatment options

Who Should Be Your Healthcare Agent

Choose someone who:

  • Is willing to serve in this role
  • Lives nearby and can be reached quickly
  • Understands your values and wishes
  • Can make difficult decisions under pressure
  • Is not afraid to ask doctors questions or challenge recommendations

Implementation Guide

After Completing Your Documents

  1. Review with attorney: Have an estate planning attorney review your documents to ensure compliance with state law
  2. Make copies: Create multiple copies - one for yourself, one for your healthcare agent, one for your medical records
  3. Store securely: Keep originals in a secure but accessible location (not a safe deposit box your agent cannot access)
  4. Give to healthcare agent: Provide your agent with a copy and discuss your wishes in detail
  5. Notify your doctor: Give a copy to your primary care physician to include in your medical file
  6. Carry wallet card: Keep a small wallet card indicating you have healthcare directives and where they are located

Review and Update

Review your healthcare directives:

  • After major life events (marriage, divorce, new child, death of family member)
  • When your health status changes significantly
  • When you move to a different state
  • Every 3-5 years as a general rule
Key Points to Remember

Healthcare directives only take effect when you cannot make decisions yourself. While you are capable, you always retain the right to make your own medical decisions, regardless of what your directive says. These documents are backups for unexpected circumstances.
