Why Business Owners Need Special Planning

The Unique Challenges

Business owners face estate planning challenges that differ significantly from individuals without business interests. Your business may represent your life's work, your employees' livelihoods, and your family's financial security.

Critical Statistics

Studies show that over 60% of business owners do not have a succession plan. Without proper planning, 70% of businesses that change hands due to death or disability fail within five years. The surviving family often faces a choice between accepting an unwanted partner, selling at a steep discount, or liquidating.

What Can Go Wrong Without Planning

  • Forced sale: Your estate may be forced to sell the business at below-market value to pay estate taxes
  • Disputes: Co-owners and family members may fight over the business
  • Loss of key relationships: Customers, vendors, and employees may leave during uncertainty
  • Tax catastrophe: Estate taxes can consume 40%+ of the business value
  • Business interruption: No one knows who should make decisions
  • Compensation issues: Heirs may expect salaries for not working

Key Documents Needed

  • Buy-sell agreement
  • Business succession plan
  • Updated business will/trust
  • Key person insurance
  • Operating/shareholder agreements
  • Business power of attorney

Professional Team Required

  • Estate planning attorney
  • Business attorney
  • Accountant/CPA
  • Financial advisor
  • Insurance specialist
  • Business broker (for valuation)

Section 6166: Estate Tax Installment

For closely held businesses, the IRS allows estate taxes to be paid in installments over 15 years if the business qualifies. This can provide critical cash flow during a difficult transition period. Qualifying requires the business to comprise more than 35% of the adjusted gross estate.

Valuation Discounts and Opportunities

  • Minority discounts: Non-controlling interests may qualify for 20-40% discounts
  • Lack of marketability: Private company interests are typically discounted 20-35%
  • Key person discounts: If the business depends heavily on you, valuation may be reduced
  • QSBS (Section 1202): C corporations with qualified small business stock may be 100% excluded from federal capital gains

Buy-Sell Agreements

What Is a Buy-Sell Agreement?

A buy-sell agreement is a binding contract between business owners that controls what happens to each owner's interest when certain triggering events occur (death, disability, retirement, divorce, bankruptcy, etc.). It ensures an orderly transition while protecting all parties.

Key Buy-Sell Agreement Components

  • Triggering events: Define what circumstances activate the agreement
  • Purchase price: How business value is determined
  • Funding mechanism: How the purchase will be funded
  • Purchase options: Who has the right to buy and in what order
  • Transfer restrictions: Limits on transferring interests to third parties
  • Obligations: What the departing owner (or estate) must do

Funding Methods

  • Life insurance: Most common for death scenarios
  • Disability insurance: For incapacity events
  • Capital accumulation: Set aside funds over time
  • Installment payments: Pay over time from business cash flow
  • Lender financing: Business borrows to fund purchase

Valuation Methods

  • Fixed price: Specific dollar amount, updated periodically
  • Book value: Based on balance sheet
  • Appraisal formula: Based on earnings multiple, revenue, or assets
  • Independent appraisal: Professional valuation at triggering event
  • Stated formula: Agreed mathematical formula
Critical Warning

A buy-sell agreement is only effective if it is properly funded. Having an agreement but no funding mechanism is like having insurance with no premium paid. Work with an insurance specialist to ensure adequate coverage for your buy-sell structure.

Gradual vs. Immediate Transfer

Research shows that gradual ownership transfers are more successful than sudden transfers. Consider a multi-year transition where you gradually transfer ownership while the successor assumes increasing management responsibility. This allows time for knowledge transfer, relationship building, and course correction if issues arise.

Family Business Considerations

  • Fair vs. equal: Decide whether assets should be divided equally among children or fairly (not necessarily equally) based on their involvement
  • Non-participating family members: Have a clear policy on what family members who don't work in the business receive
  • Compensation for family: Family members should be paid fair market compensation for work performed
  • Professional management: Consider whether non-family professional managers are appropriate
  • Family council: Establish a family governance structure separate from business management

Key Person Protection and Insurance

Key Person Insurance

Key person insurance provides a death benefit to the business when a critical employee dies. This money can be used to recruit a replacement, cover lost profits during transition, or repay debts that came due.

When You Are the Key Person

  • The business may struggle without you
  • Business value may be tied to your relationships and expertise
  • Surviving owners need funds to continue
  • Your estate needs liquidity to pay expenses
  • Your death may trigger partner buyout

Coverage Types

  • Term life: Affordable, simple protection for known needs
  • Permanent life: Cash value + coverage, more expensive
  • Disability income: Replaces income if you cannot work
  • Disability buy-out: Funds partner buyout if you're disabled
  • Business overhead expense: Covers fixed costs during disability

Business Continuation Planning

  • Document critical relationships: List key customers, vendors, and what they mean to the business
  • Create succession documentation: Who does what, how, and why
  • Cross-train employees: No single person should be irreplaceable
  • Digital assets: Ensure passwords, contracts, and files are accessible
  • Emergency contacts: Have contact information for all advisors

Essential Business Documents Checklist

  • [ ] Business Succession Plan
  • [ ] Key Person Insurance Policies

Business Wills and Trusts

Your Business Interest in Your Personal Estate Plan

Your will and personal estate planning documents should address your business interest. This includes who inherits your business, how it should be managed after your death, and any restrictions you want to place on transfer.

Key Provisions for Business Owners

  • Business disposition: Direct whether your interest goes to family, co-owners, or others
  • Management instructions: Provide guidance on running the business
  • Buy-sell coordination: Ensure your estate plan works with the buy-sell agreement
  • Tag-along/drag-along rights: Specify what happens to minority interests
  • Capital account treatment: Direct how your capital account is distributed
  • Dispute resolution: Specify how business disputes should be resolved
Business Trusts

Consider placing your business interest in a trust for estate planning benefits. A properly structured grantor trust can remove business assets from your taxable estate while allowing you to maintain control during your lifetime. Consult with an estate planning attorney to determine if this strategy is appropriate for your situation.

Integration with Buy-Sell Agreement

Your estate plan and buy-sell agreement must work together:

  • The buy-sell agreement determines the transaction when you die
  • Your will should direct that your estate participates in the buy-sell
  • Life insurance proceeds should flow to fund the buyout
  • If you are a surviving owner, your estate plan should reflect your buy-sell rights
  • Regular coordination between documents is essential as values change

Implementation Roadmap

12-Month Business Estate Planning Checklist

  • Month 1-2: Assess current documents and identify gaps
  • Month 2-3: Meet with business attorney to review operating agreement
  • Month 3-4: Meet with estate planning attorney about personal plan
  • Month 4-5: Review or create buy-sell agreement with funding
  • Month 5-6: Meet with insurance specialist to evaluate coverage
  • Month 6-8: Get business valuation for buy-sell and estate planning
  • Month 8-9: Create or update business succession plan
  • Month 9-10: Update personal will/trust to reflect business interests
  • Month 10-11: Review beneficiary designations on retirement accounts and insurance
  • Month 11-12: Final review with entire professional team
Annual Review Required

Business estate planning should be reviewed annually and after major events including business sales or purchases, significant changes in business value, family changes (births, deaths, divorces), changes in business structure, and major financial events.

Professional Help Is Essential

Business estate planning is complex and requires professional guidance. The cost of proper planning is minimal compared to the potential costs of improper planning: family disputes, forced sales, tax penalties, and business failure. Work with a coordinated team including business attorney, estate planning attorney, accountant, and financial advisor. This guide provides education only.
